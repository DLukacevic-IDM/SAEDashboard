# Domain Knowledge

## What is SAE?

**Subnational Area Estimation (SAE)** is a statistical methodology for producing reliable estimates of health indicators at fine geographic levels (regions, districts) where direct survey data may be sparse. SAE combines survey data (e.g., DHS — Demographic and Health Surveys) with spatial models to generate predictions with uncertainty bounds for every administrative unit.

This dashboard was developed by the **Institute for Disease Modeling (IDM)** to make SAE model outputs accessible to policymakers, researchers, and program managers working on public health in sub-Saharan Africa.

## Why This Dashboard Exists

National-level health statistics often mask significant subnational variation. A country may report 30% modern contraceptive prevalence nationally, but individual districts may range from 5% to 60%. Without subnational visibility:

- Resources get allocated uniformly instead of where need is greatest
- Progress in some areas can mask stagnation in others
- Targeted interventions cannot be designed or evaluated

The SAE Dashboard solves this by visualizing model-estimated indicators at **region (Admin Level 1)** and **district (Admin Level 2)** resolution, with time-series trends and uncertainty quantification.

## Family Planning Indicators (Senegal Dataset)

The sample dataset tracks three core family planning indicators among women of reproductive age (15-49) in Senegal:

| Indicator | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **modern_method** | Prevalence of modern contraceptive methods (pills, IUDs, implants, injectables, condoms, sterilization) |
| **traditional_method** | Prevalence of traditional contraceptive methods (rhythm, withdrawal, folk methods) |
| **unmet_need** | Proportion of women who want to delay or stop childbearing but are not using any contraceptive method |

These indicators are central to monitoring **Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 3.7** — universal access to sexual and reproductive health services.

## Disaggregation

Each indicator is broken down by demographic subgroups to reveal inequities:

| Dimension | Subgroups |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Age** | 15-24 (younger women), 25+ (older women), all |
| **Parity** | Parity-0 (no children), Parity-1plus (one or more children) |
| **Residence** | Urban, Rural |
| **Combinations** | e.g., 15-24_Parity-0, 25plus_urban, 15-24_rural |

This produces **13 subgroup files per indicator** (39 total CSV files for 3 indicators), enabling analysis like: *"Is modern method uptake among young urban women in Diourbel improving faster than among rural women in Kédougou?"*

## Geographic Hierarchy

Senegal's administrative structure in the dashboard:

```
Africa (continent)
└── Senegal (country) ─── 14 regions (Admin Level 1)
    ├── Dakar ─── 4 districts (Admin Level 2)
    │   ├── Dakar, Guédiawaye, Pikine, Rufisque
    ├── Diourbel ─── 3 districts
    │   ├── Bambey, Diourbel, Mbacké
    ├── Fatick, Kaffrine, Kaolack, Kédougou, Kolda,
    │   Louga, Matam, Saint-Louis, Sédhiou, Tambacounda,
    │   Thiès, Ziguinchor
    └── (~45 districts total)
```

Regions and districts are identified using **dot names** — colon-separated hierarchical identifiers:
- `Africa:Senegal` (country level)
- `Africa:Senegal:Dakar` (region level)
- `Africa:Senegal:Dakar:Pikine` (district level)

## Data Provenance

- **Model estimates**: Generated by IDM's spatial models combining DHS survey microdata with covariates
- **Reference values**: Direct DHS survey estimates shown alongside model predictions for validation
- **Uncertainty bounds**: 95% credible intervals derived from model posterior distributions
- **Geographic boundaries**: GADM v4.1 administrative boundary data (gadm.org)
- **Time coverage**: ~1990–2020+ depending on indicator and data availability
